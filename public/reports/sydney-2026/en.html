Sydney's student market is living through a value reset

In June 2026, uhomes surveyed its study-abroad channel partners across China in two waves. 218 frontline responses map how diversion, price comparison and a cooling cycle are reshaping demand for Sydney — and how the economics of student housing are being recalculated.

218valid responses (75 self-completed + 143 interviews)
78.7%of respondents are study-abroad agencies
30+cities covered across China
Jun 7–11fieldwork window, 2026
55–65%of partners report year-on-year decline in Sydney enquiries (cross-wave range)
9.8%of interviewed partners expect recovery within 3 months
57.3%will shift resources to Melbourne over the next 6–12 months
72%name "extreme rent levels" as students' top housing complaint
01

The demand thermometer: decline is the consensus

Two independent waves used different scales, yet point the same way: versus last year and the 2025 fall intake, Sydney enquiries and signings are softening — and face-to-face interviews read distinctly colder.

How to read this

Anonymous self-reports put the decline share near 58.7%; face-to-face interviews push it to 66.5% — partners speak more candidly in person. Triangulating both waves, the true decline share sits between 55% and 65%. And this is an enquiry-side signal: by industry lag, it reaches the accommodation side roughly a year later.

Structural, not cyclical

One equation kept recurring in interviews: most Australian master's degrees run two years; the UK, Hong Kong and Singapore run one — roughly double the cost and an extra year. That is not sentiment; the destination value calculus itself has shifted.

Most Australian master's programmes take two years; the UK, Hong Kong and Singapore take one. Families are doing that maths more carefully than ever.

The mechanics of diversion

Several partners described the same scene: a student already holding a Sydney CoE switches after a top Melbourne university lowers entry requirements and grants credit exemptions. With Queensland warming too, Australia's internal demand map is being redrawn. For partners committed to Australia, this is a structural reallocation window, not simple shrinkage.

The moment Melbourne dropped its entry bar, Sydney followed — but the spotlight was gone. The student's CoE was already issued, and they still switched to Melbourne.

05

What students actually worry about

After the offer lands, students' biggest anxiety is not where they will live — it is where they will end up after graduation. That should shape how housing is sold: inside a narrative of total cost and certainty.

Implication for the conversation

Housing ranks only fifth on the anxiety list (33.3%) yet rent tops the complaint list (72%). In other words: accommodation is not students' biggest fear, but it is the biggest variable in their budget. Real housing-cost data inside a total-cost comparison persuades better than any room brochure.

06

Outlook — and what we will do next

The base case is "bumping along the bottom", not a rebound: 49.3% of Wave A expect six more flat months and only 18.7% see recovery. A cool market is exactly when fundamentals are built. Based on this survey, we commit to:

A Sydney exclusive-deals programme

Exclusive offers and early-bird reductions for Sydney institutions — the single most requested support in both waves (64% / 56.6%), and the most direct answer to the number-one pain point: rent.

A campus housing & living guide for Sydney

Real rent levels, commute times and living costs around every major campus — turning information asymmetry into decision-ready material partners can hand straight to families. 40% of partners asked for exactly this.

Faster response, clearer settlement

A dedicated Sydney case-handling lane and more transparent partner settlement — so every referral comes with a predictable experience.

Following the market into Melbourne

As demand migrates to Melbourne and Queensland, our inventory operations and partner programmes will move with it — equal-strength support in the growth cities.

Sydney will come good again — the housing demand is there.
