> This is the blog edition of the uhomes Sydney channel survey, June 2026. The full interactive report: [English](/reports/sydney-2026/en.html) · [中文版](/reports/sydney-2026/cn.html). A Chinese edition of this post is [here](/blog/sydney-student-market-survey-2026).

In early June, we ran a two-wave survey of our study-abroad channel partners across China: an anonymous self-completed questionnaire (75 responses) and one-on-one interviews by our BD team (143), for 218 frontline data points across 30+ cities. Nearly 80% of respondents are study-abroad agencies.

The trigger was simple: Sydney leads had visibly softened this year, and I wanted to know whether that was a cycle or something else.

It's something else. But before the market, one methodological surprise.

## The anonymous form and the interview gave different answers

The same question — "How does Sydney enquiry demand compare with the 2025 fall intake?" — produced a gap of almost eight percentage points between waves:

- Anonymous self-completed: about **58.7%** reported decline
- Face-to-face interviews: **66.5%** reported decline, with "marked decline" alone at 37.8%

![Sydney enquiry demand vs the 2025 fall intake](/images/blog/sydney-2026/demand-en.svg)

I expected the opposite — anonymity is supposed to invite honesty. In practice, **partners complain more candidly in person**; on anonymous forms they drift toward the mild option.

The lesson: never trust a single collection channel. Triangulating both waves, we put the true decline share at **55–65%**. If you run surveys, run at least two collection modes — the gap between them is itself information.

## The root cause isn't visas — the value calculus changed

Ask "why the decline" and the ranking speaks for itself. Tighter visas come only fifth:

![Why partners think Sydney demand is falling](/images/blog/sydney-2026/causes-en.svg)

The top two: diversion to Hong Kong and Singapore, and shrinking family budgets. One equation kept recurring in the interview free-text:

> Most Australian master's programmes take two years; the UK, Hong Kong and Singapore take one. Families are doing that maths more carefully than ever.

That isn't sentiment — it's structure. **At equal budgets, the destination value calculus has shifted**, and Sydney happens to be one of the highest-priced assets being repriced.

## Within Australia, Melbourne is pulling ahead

The internal diversion is even more interesting. Asked which Australian city they will prioritise over the next 6–12 months, partners answered in one voice:

![Where partners will shift resources, next 6-12 months](/images/blog/sydney-2026/cities-en.svg)

Several partners described the same scene: a student already holding a Sydney CoE switches the moment a top Melbourne university lowers its entry bar and grants credit exemptions.

Sydney's loss is not Australia's loss — **the demand hasn't vanished; it's being redistributed across the map**. For anyone running Australia operations, that's a reallocation window, not plain shrinkage.

## Housing: the pain moved from "can't get one" to "can't afford one"

Accommodation is our core business, so I read this section most closely.

On supply and demand, Sydney renting is no longer the frenzy of the past two years: 65.3% of partners say the market is broadly balanced, and 20% say renting has become easier. Price is a different story:

![Students' loudest Sydney housing complaints](/images/blog/sydney-2026/housing-en.svg)

72% of partners name rent as students' loudest complaint — 35 points clear of second place. The pain didn't disappear; it changed shape: **from "can't win a room" to "can't afford one"**.

## What students actually worry about: housing ranks fifth, but rent is the biggest variable

This last chart changed how we design our messaging:

![Students' top post-offer anxieties](/images/blog/sydney-2026/anxieties-en.svg)

After the offer lands, students worry most about post-graduation jobs (68%) and visas (57.3%). Housing ranks only fifth (33.3%).

Put that against the complaint chart above and the conclusion writes itself: **accommodation is not students' biggest fear, but it is the biggest variable in their budget**. Selling rooms is inefficient; putting real housing-cost data inside a total-cost-of-study comparison is what families actually need.

## One leading indicator, to be checked in 2027

One partner said something in the interviews that stuck with me:

> The application side feels the market first; it takes about a year to reach the housing side.

By that lag, this year's 55–65% decline in enquiries should show up in Sydney check-in data in early 2027. I'm writing it down here so we can come back and mark the answer.
